* The Finish Line Strategy: Crafting Your Case from Verdict to Trial

How to Build a Case Backward from the Finish Line

When a new case walks through the door, it is tempting to start at the
beginning—with  intake notes,  facts, and  client interviews.  But the
truth is that the first step in evaluating any case is to start at the
end. Picture  yourself standing  before the jury  or judge  in closing
argument. What  are you saying?  How are you  asking them to  find for
your client?  What words will they  see on the verdict  form? And what
jury instructions will guide their deliberations?

Trey Gowdy reminds us that “chance favors the prepared mind.”  Nowhere
is that more  true than in case evaluation. Before  deciding to take a
case – or at  least be willing to take a case to  trial, you must know
what the final jury instructions are going to look like. That requires
thorough  research  of legal  theories,  causes  of action,  defenses,
and—most  importantly—what the  judge  or jury  must actually  decide.
Without that, you  cannot develop your winning theme  or strategies or
fairly assess the chances of winning.

Robert  Burns,  in A  Theory  of  the  Trial,  calls trial  “a  public
enactment of  a moral drama.   ” But you  can’t stage a  drama without
knowing  its  final   act.  Charles  Rose,  who   writes  and  teaches
extensively on trial  storytelling, reminds us that  narrative is only
satisfying when it ends with resolution. And Robert Klein, a master of
jury persuasion,  often advises that  if you  cannot see how  you will
explain the  verdict in closing,  you should think twice  about taking
the case at all.

** Step One -- Picture the Closing Argument

Before you take  the case, pause and  imagine what you want  to say in
your last five minutes with the  jury. Don’t worry yet about polish or
detail—that comes later. What matters at the outset is whether you can
see an ending that makes sense, fits the law, and feels just – and one
that you can get  behind. If you can’t picture that, it  may be a sign
to think twice about proceeding.

** Step Two -- Study the Jury Instructions

Jury  instructions are  the law’s  distillation. They  are the  script
jurors must follow in the deliberation room. Francis Wellman warned in
The  Art of  Cross- Examination  against  asking a  HEADER (CHANGE  ON
MASTER B)  question without knowing  the answer;  the same is  true of
case selection: never  take a case unless you know  what the jury will
be told at the end.”

That means reading pattern instructions  early, researching the law in
cases without  pattern instructions and consider  alternative theories
if one  path appears  blocked. In federal  court, the  Ninth Circuit’s
model  instructions  are  available  at  the click  of  a  button.  In
Washington  and  Oregon state  courts,  the  pattern instructions  are
equally essential. Know them before you commit yourself.

** Step Three -- Research the Legal Theories

Too many lawyers fall in love with facts without first testing whether
the law can  carry them. Before you invest in  discovery, experts, and
years of work, research the legal theories thoroughly.

- Does the cause of action exist under your state’s law?
- Is there precedent cutting against you?
- Are damages capped or constrained?
- What defenses will be automatic, and how strong are they?

Robert Klein has  often said that the strongest case  is one where the
law, the facts, and the equities all  line up. If one leg of the stool
is  missing,  the  case  may  topple no  matter  how  passionate  your
advocacy.

** Step Four -- See the Verdict Form

Before you  take the case,  ask yourself:  what does the  verdict form
look  like? In  civil cases,  will the  jury be  asked for  liability,
damages, or both?  In criminal cases, is the burden  of proof as clear
as your client believes?

Charles Rose teaches that the verdict form is not just a legal tool—it
is the final scene in your story. The jurors must be able to check the
boxes in a way that feels  morally and narratively satisfying. If your
theory doesn’t line up with the verdict form, you are setting yourself
up for a fractured, incoherent trial.

** Step Five -- Weigh the Chances Honestly

Starting at the finish line forces you to be honest.  Sometimes you’ll
realize early that  the jury instructions will  be insurmountable, the
verdict form unhelpful,  or the law too thin. Better  to know that now
than three years down the road.

This isn’t pessimism;  it’s preparation. It’s about  stacking the odds
in your client’s favor by doing the hard work of evaluation up front.

** Conclusion -- Seeing the End Before the Beginning

Great trial lawyers don’t wander  into the courtroom hoping to “figure
it out  as they  go.”  They  start at  the finish  line. They  see the
closing argument, the  jury instructions, and the  verdict form before
they ever file  the complaint or answer.  That  foresight shapes every
step that follows—from discovery, to witnesses, to trial themes.

Experience has taught  me, sometimes the hard way, that  cases are won
or lost long before the trial begins. By starting at the end, you give
yourself  the only  real chance  to  shape a  story, a  theory, and  a
verdict that can withstand the scrutiny of judge and jury alike.

* The Will to Win Is Not Nearly As Important As the Will to Prepare to Win

Chance favors the prepared mind.

Rick Friedman in _Becoming a Trial Lawyer_, reminds us that he has never
considered  himself  a  great  speaker—he describes  himself  as  shy,
sometimes  awkward—but  he  has  won extraordinary  cases  because  he
prepares relentlessly.  He leaves nothing to chance.

** Purpose-driven Litigation and Discovery Plan

Preparation is not about doing everything. It is about doing the right
things, with purpose, again and again.

Too  often, lawyers  confuse  activity with  preparation— burying  the
other side  in paperwork,  scheduling depositions  without a  plan, or
demanding every possible document “just in case” — which mostly wastes
time, irritates judges, and incurs excessive costs for clients.

In  his book,  Winning  Your  Jury, Robert  Klein  teaches that  trial
lawyers must plan their cases like military campaigns—choosing battles
carefully, conserving resources, and always remembering the objective.

Charles Rose  in Advocacy Case Files,  reminds us that every  piece of
discovery should  serve the story  you are  building for trial.  If it
doesn’t, it’s a distraction.

** The Battle Plan

Know what weapons you have in your arsenal, but don’t use all of them:

- Witness Interviews :: Talk to every witness who matters. Don’t just collect names—collect stories, contradictions, and context.

- Subpoenas ::  Think carefully  about what records  you will  need to
  prove your story. Subpoena early so you’re not scrambling on the eve
  of trial.

- Depositions      ::      Discovery   depositions   are   not   about
  box-checking. They are  about sculpting the testimony  you’ll use at
  trial.  Francis  Wellman would  say: never ask  a question  at trial
  without knowing the answer—so make sure you learn the answers now.

- Requests for Admissions/motions for partial summary judgment :: These
  are  underused but  powerful. Force  your opponent  to concede  what
  should not be disputed, so the trial is about what matters.

- Experts ::  Read everything your  expert has written  and everything
  about the opposing  expert. Cross- examination of experts  is won in
  the library, not in the courtroom.

- Documents ::  Read the  record—twice. Then read  it again.  Too many
  lawyers  walk into  trial never  having studied  their own  exhibits
  carefully.

** Facts Are the Ammunition

Trials   are  not   won  by   rhetoric  alone.   They  are   won  with
facts—carefully  gathered,  organized,  and   deployed  at  the  right
time. Robert  Burns reminds  us that  trial is  moral drama,  but even
drama requires  props, dialogue, and  details. Preparation is  how you
make sure the facts you need are in the record, ready to be used.

The best closing arguments are  not improvised—they are constructed on
the  foundation of  facts you  have spent  months gathering.  The best
cross- examinations are not witty  retorts—they are the culmination of
depositions, research, and planning.

** The Discipline to Prepare

Abraham Lincoln  put it this  way: “Give me six  hours to chop  down a
tree and I will spend the first four sharpening the axe.”

Preparation is  not easy.  It takes discipline  to keep  reading, keep
questioning, keep drafting, keep planning.  It is easier to assume you
already know  enough.  But  the difference  between “good  enough” and
“fully  prepared”   is  often  the  difference   between  winning  and
losing. The jury will only follow your  trail to a just verdict if you
can demonstrate that you have thoroughly scoped out the forest.

Rick Friedman proves  this point: He wins because he  refuses to leave
stones unturned; relentless preparation and having fully exhausted all
avenues of  truth.  As he has  put it, “Preparation is  what allows an
ordinary lawyer to do extraordinary things.”

** Conclusion: Sharpening the Axe

The will to win makes us competitive.  But, the will to prepare to win
makes us effective when it matters  most -when the jury walks into the
box and the judge gavels in the trial.

The lesson  for trial lawyers  is clear.  Winning is not  always about
charisma or theatrics. It is about preparation—the quiet, disciplined,
sometimes tedious work  of gathering facts, mastering  the record, and
building a  battle plan.  That is what  turns ordinary  advocates into
solid if not extraordinary trial lawyers.
