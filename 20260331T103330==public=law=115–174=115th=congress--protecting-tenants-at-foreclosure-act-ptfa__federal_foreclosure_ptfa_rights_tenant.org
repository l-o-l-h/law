* Overview

The Protecting Tenants at Foreclosure Act (PTFA), permanently restored
by Pub. L. 115–174 on May  24, 2018, requires new owners of foreclosed
residential properties to honor existing  bona fide leases and provide
at  least 90  days'  notice  before requiring  tenants  to vacate.  It
applies  to  all  residential foreclosures,  protecting  tenants  with
leases until the end of their  term and month-to-month tenants with 90
days' notice.

The law  was initially passed in  2009 in response to  the foreclosure
crisis and was brought back into effect in 2018 to ensure tenants have
sufficient time to find alternative housing

The Economic  Growth, Regulatory  Relief, and Consumer  Protection Act
(Economic Growth  Act) repealed the PTFA’s  sunset date as of  30 days
after the Economic Growth Act’s enactment. As a result, the PTFA is in
effect permanently as of June 23, 2018.

* Application

The PTFA applies to
- any foreclosure on a federally related mortgage loan,5 (12 USC 5220 note)
- dwelling, or residential real property
- (collectively, PTFA-covered foreclosures).6

The PTFA provides that any immediate successor in interest7 in such a
foreclosed property, including a bank that takes complete title to a
house upon foreclosure,
- assumes the interest subject to the rights of any bona fide tenant
  and
- needs to comply with certain notice requirements.

* Key components of the law include:

- Permanent Status :: The 2018 legislation repealed previous sunset
  clauses, making the protections permanent.

- Bona Fide Lease Protection :: Tenants with a lease must be allowed
  to occupy the property until the end of their lease term.

- 90-Day Notice :: If no lease exists or the unit is sold to someone
  who will occupy it as a primary residence, the tenant is entitled to
  a minimum of 90 days' written notice to vacate.

- Section 8 Protections :: For Section 8 tenants, the successor in
  interest must assume the housing assistance payment contract and the
  lease.

- Scope :: The law applies to all foreclosures on federally related
  mortgage loans or any residential property.

- State Law :: The PTFA does not override more protective state or
  local laws.

* Lack of Codification Summary

The Protecting Tenants at Foreclosure Act (PTFA) is not codified as a
standalone section in the United States Code.

Instead, the PTFA's operative provisions exist as uncodified notes
appended to existing U.S. Code sections—specifically as notes to
- 12 U.S.C. § 5201,
- 12 U.S.C. § 5220, and
- 42 U.S.C. § 1437f.

The law's substantive text is referenced in and through its enacting
legislation:
- originally Pub.  L.  111-22 (the Helping Families Save Their Homes
  Act of 2009)
- and currently Pub. L.  115-174 (the Economic Growth, Regulatory
  Relief, and Consumer Protection Act of 2018), which permanently
  restored the PTFA.

Courts and practitioners must look to those public laws and the
Statutes at Large, rather than to a numbered U.S. Code section, to
find the PTFA's operative text.

** Legislative History

The PTFA was originally enacted as Title VII (§§ 701–704) of the Helping Families Save Their Homes Act of 2009, Pub. L. No. 111-22, 123 Stat. 1632, 1660–62 (May 20, 2009) PL 111-22, May 20, 2009, 123 Stat 1632.

The Ninth Circuit in _Logan v. U.S. Bank Nat. Ass'n_, 722 F.3d 1163 (2013) confirmed this statutory lineage, describing the PTFA as "Pub.L. No. 111–22, § 701–04, 123 Stat. 1632, 1660–62 (2009)" and explaining that it "was enacted as part of the Helping Families Save Their Homes Act of 2009 ('the Homes Act')," with the PTFA appearing as "Title VII, §§ 701–04" of that larger act. _Logan v. U.S. Bank Nat. Ass'n_, 722 F.3d 1163 (2013).

The original PTFA contained four sections:
- § 701 (short title),
- § 702 (effect of foreclosure on preexisting tenancy),
- § 703 (effect of foreclosure on Section 8 tenancies), and
- § 704 (sunset provision).
  - The original sunset provision in § 704 set the PTFA to expire on December 31, 2012, and the statute was later interpreted to have lapsed by December 31, 2014 PL 111-22, May 20, 2009, 123 Stat 163.2

** Codification as a Note, Not a Standalone Code Section

The PTFA's enacting legislation itself reveals the statute's uncodified status.

- The enacted text of § 702 is marked with the annotation "<< 12 USCA
  § 5220 NOTE >>," indicating it was placed as a note to 12 U.S.C. §
  5220 rather than given its own numbered section in the Code PL
  111-22, May 20, 2009, 123 Stat 1632.

- Similarly, §§ 701 and 704 carry the notation "<< 12 USCA § 5201 NOTE
  >>,"

- and § 703's amendments to Section 8 housing law are noted at "<< 42
  USCA § 1437f NOTE >>" PL 111-22, May 20, 2009, 123 Stat 1632.


This means the PTFA does not occupy an independent, numbered title or
section in the United States Code. Researchers cannot look up "12
U.S.C. § [X]" and find the PTFA's tenant-protection text
directly. Instead, the PTFA's provisions are accessible through
annotations to other Code sections, and the full operative text is
found in the Statutes at Large under Pub. L. 111-22.

Courts have consistently reflected this status by citing the PTFA
through its public law and Statutes at Large references rather than
through standalone U.S. Code sections.

** The Dodd-Frank Amendment (2010)

In 2010, the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection
Act, Pub. L. 111-203, § 1484, 124 Stat. 1376, 2204, amended the PTFA
to clarify its provisions and extend its application. _Logan
v. U.S. Bank Nat. Ass'n_, 722 F.3d 1163 (2013).

Notably, this amendment did not elevate the PTFA to a formally
codified U.S. Code section; it modified the existing note-level
provisions in place.

** The 2018 Permanent Restoration

After the PTFA's expiration, Congress permanently restored it through § 304 of the Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act, Pub. L. 115-174, 132 Stat. 1296 (May 24, 2018), effective June 23, 2018 (30 days after enactment) PL 115-174, May 24, 2018, 132 Stat 1296.

Section 304 operates in two parts:

(a) the repeal of the PTFA's sunset provision, which was identified as "Section 704 of the Protecting Tenants at Foreclosure Act of 2009 (12 U.S.C. 5201 note; 12 U.S.C. 5220 note; 42 U.S.C. 1437f note)," and

(b) the restoration of §§ 701 through 703 of the PTFA, along with any amendments and regulations that were in effect on December 30, 2014 PL 115-174, May 24, 2018, 132 Stat 1296.

Critically, the 2018 Act's parenthetical identification of the PTFA as existing at "12 U.S.C. 5201 note; 12 U.S.C. 5220 note; 42 U.S.C. 1437f note" PL 115-174, May 24, 2018, 132 Stat 1296 constitutes a congressional confirmation that the PTFA resides in note status. The 2018 restoration did not re-codify the PTFA as substantive law with its own Code section number—it simply removed the sunset and declared the previously enacted (note-level) provisions to be alive again.

** Practical Implications

Because the PTFA is uncodified, practitioners and courts working with PTFA issues must locate its text in the Statutes at Large or through notes attached to 12 U.S.C. § 5220 and related sections, rather than by citation to a standalone United States Code section.

In _Logan v. U.S. Bank Nat. Ass'n_, 722 F.3d 1163 (2013), the Ninth Circuit held that the PTFA provides no private right of action, noting that the statute's focus on regulated parties, its framing in terms of "protections" for tenants, and the absence of explicit enforcement language suggested it was "intended to provide a defense in state eviction proceedings rather than a basis for offensive suits in federal court."

In _Kang v. Marathon Funding Services, Inc._, Not Reported in Fed. Supp. (2019), the Western District of Washington held that a defendant's invocation of the PTFA as a defense to an unlawful detainer action did not create federal question jurisdiction, because the PTFA defense was not the basis of the plaintiff's claims. These results reflect the practical limits arising from the PTFA's structure and purpose.

** Commentary on This Question

The Protecting Tenants at Foreclosure Act of 2009 (PTFA), enacted as a federal statute under the Helping Families Save Their Homes Act of 2009, protects tenants in foreclosed residential properties by requiring successor owners to honor existing leases or give a minimum of 90 days’ notice to vacate for tenants at will or month-to-month tenants. The PTFA applies broadly to federally related mortgage loans and addresses tenants occupying dwellings with bona fide leases, excluding occupants paying substantially less than fair market value or the mortgagor themselves. The Act was originally temporary but was made permanent in 2018. While codified within the United States Code, the Act is referenced as a federal statute within notes appended to relevant sections of Title 12 and Title 42 USC, particularly 12 U.S.C. § 5201 note, 12 U.S.C. § 5220 note, and 42 U.S.C. § 1437f note. This codification as notes indicates that the protections are contained within the broader statutory context rather than in an independent, stand-alone codified section. States including Washington are generally subject to this federal statute as a matter of federal law, though state-specific variations or additional protections may apply.
