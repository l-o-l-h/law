* Motion to Enforce a Settlement

“Where  the  CR  2A  requirements  are met,  a  motion  to  enforce  a
settlement is  a commonly  accepted practice.”
- _Condon v.  Condon_, 177 Wn.2d 150,  157, 298  P.3d 86  (2013).

The party moving  to enforce a settlement agreement has  the burden of
proving that no genuine dispute exists over the existence and material
terms  of the  agreement.
- _Brinkerhoff  v. Campbell_,  99 Wn.  App. 692, 696-97,  994 P.2d  911 (2000)
- (citing _In  re Marriage  of Ferree_,  71 Wash.App. 35, 41, 856 P.2d 706  (1993)).

If  a  dispute  of  material  fact exists,  the  court  must  hold  an
evidentiary hearing.
- Id.


Generally, the _Marriage of Ferree_ opinion  seems to be the basis for a
lot of the litigation about CR 2A  agreements. So, if you need to find
some nuance of this  body of law, I’d look for  cases that cite Ferree
and then search within that universe.


If the agreement does not satisfy  the requirements of CR 2A, then the
agreement is  just a  contract that  must be  enforced like  any other
contract.


* What are the differences betweeen a CR2A and "just a contract"?

Under Washington law, the primary substantive difference between a *CR
2A agreement*  and a *regular  contract* lies in the:
- *evidentiary* and *procedural* requirements
- for *enforceability*
- rather than the underlying legal theory.

Washington courts have  long held that "standard  contract law governs
whether the parties  have reached a settlement"
- (*Stottlemyre v. Reed*, 1983).

However,  CR  2A  imposes  a  "statute  of  frauds"  type  of  overlay
specifically for agreements made during litigation.

** 1. Evidentiary Thresholds

A regular contract  can often be proven through a  combination of oral
testimony,  course  of  conduct,  and partial  performance.  A  CR  2A
agreement, however, is strictly limited  by *Superior Court Civil Rule
2A*.

*** *The "Writing or Record" Requirement

A court will  not recognize a settlement agreement if  its "purport is
disputed" unless it is:
  1. *In  writing* and signed by  the party (or their  attorney) against
     whom it is being enforced; OR
  2. *Made in open court* and assented to on the record.


*** *Purpose:*

The rule is designed to prevent "swearing contests" (he-said/she-said)
regarding the  existence or terms  of a settlement reached  during the
heat of litigation or mediation.

** 2. Enforcement Procedure

The way you "sue" on these documents differs significantly.

| Feature            | Regular Contract                                                         | CR 2A Agreement                                                                                  |
|--------------------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Action Required    | Typically requires filing a new lawsuit for breach of contract.          | Can be enforced via motion practice within the existing lawsuit.                                 |
|                    |                                                                          |                                                                                                  |
| Standard of Review | Summary judgment standard (de novo) if no material facts are in dispute. | Reviewed de novo;                                                                                |
|                    |                                                                          | if the terms are disputed, the court must hold an evidentiary hearing (_Brinkerhoff v. Campbell_). |
|                    |                                                                          |                                                                                                  |
| Remedy             | Damages or specific performance.                                         | Can be converted directly into a court order or judgment in the pending cause.                   |
|                    |                                                                          |                                                                                                  |
|--------------------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

** 3. Scope of Subject Matter

- *Regular Contracts ::*
  - Can cover almost any legal subject matter.

- *CR 2A Agreements ::*
  - Limited to  "proceedings in a cause."  Furthermore, parties cannot
    use a CR 2A to strip a court of its mandatory statutory duties.

- *Example ::*
  - In  family law,  a  CR  2A cannot  permanently  waive the  court’s
    authority to modify a parenting  plan based on the "best interests
    of the child" standard (*In re Marriage of Thomsen*).

** 4. Attorney Authority

Under **RCW 2.44.010**, an attorney  has specific statutory authority to
bind  a client  to  a CR  2A  agreement.  While an  agent  can bind  a
principal in regular  contract law, the signature of an  attorney on a
CR 2A is uniquely powerful and often harder to rescind by claiming the
attorney lacked  "actual authority" compared to  a standard commercial
agent.

** Summary

- Super Contract  :: A  CR 2A  agreement *is*  a contract,  but it  is a
  "super-contract" for litigation purposes.

- Strict     ::    It   is   subject  to   stricter  formation   rules
  (writing/record) but  benefits from streamlined  enforcement (motion
  vs. new lawsuit).

- Hard  to Set  Aside ::  In Washington,  while CR  2A agreements  are
  governed by standard contract law, they are much harder to set aside
  once the  procedural requirements  of the  rule are  met. Washington
  courts strongly  favor the finality  of these agreements  to prevent
  "secondary litigation" over the settlement itself.

The  following precedents  and  principles are  central to  challenges
based on mutual mistake or duress:

** Vacating a CR 2A Agreement

1. Was there a writing or record of the agreement?
2. Is there a genuine dispute over the material terms? (_Ferree_)
3. Does a common law  defense exist by clear and convincing evidence?
   - mistake
   - fraud
   - duress/coercion

*** Mutual Mistake

To set  aside a CR 2A  agreement for mutual mistake,  the moving party
faces a high evidentiary bar.

- The  Standard ::  You must  prove by  clear, cogent,  and convincing
  evidence that  both parties were  mistaken about a  basic assumption
  upon  which the  contract  was made,  and that  this  mistake had  a
  material effect on the agreed exchange (_Pepper v. Evanson_, 1967).

- The "Independent Knowledge" Rule :: If one party relies on their own
  diagnosis or investigation (e.g., their own expert's valuation of an
  asset), the mistake is often deemed unilateral rather than mutual. A
  unilateral  mistake  is  generally  insufficient to  void  a  CR  2A
  agreement unless there  is evidence of fraud  or inequitable conduct
  by the other side (_Beaver v. Estate of Harris_, 1965).

- Newly Discovered Evidence  :: In recent cases like  _Thetford v. Brim_
  (2025), the  Court of Appeals clarified  that while CR 60(b)  can be
  used to  vacate an order approving  a CR 2A agreement,  the evidence
  must be  truly "newly  discovered" and  not merely  information that
  could have been found with due diligence before signing.


**** Standard

- Burden of Proof :: Clear, cogent, convincing
- Key Precedent :: _Pepper v. Evanson_

*** Duress and Coercion

Duress is frequently alleged but rarely successful in the context of a
CR 2A agreement signed during mediation.

- The "Independent Agency" Test ::  Duress requires a showing that the
  person was "bereft of the quality of mind essential to the making of
  a contract" (In re Marriage of Ferree, 1993).

- Mediation   Pressure  vs.   Legal   Duress   ::  Washington   courts
  consistently  rule that  the natural  "stress" of  a long  mediation
  session or the "pressure" from one’s own attorney to settle does not
  constitute legal duress.

- Example Case :: In _In re Marriage of Tsane_ (2021), a party argued he
  was "bullied" into signing by  the mediator and attorneys. The court
  rejected this,  noting that when  a party is represented  by counsel
  and has  time to  review the  document, the  "duress" bar  is nearly
  impossible to  meet without  evidence of  actual illegal  threats or
  physical confinement.


**** Standard

- Burden of Proof :: Clear, cogent, convincing
- Key Precedent :: _In re Marriage of Ferree_

*** Other Defenses

**** Unconscionability

- Burden of Proof :: Clear, cogent, convincing
- Key Precedent :: _In re Marriage of Matson_

**** Fraud
