* Research question

A landlord discovers that the bathroom structure of an apartment
dwelling is severely damaged due to moisture and mold.  The damaged
conditions were not caused or created by the tenant.  The landlord
demands verbally only that the tenant must move out immediately and
utilize its own renter's insurance policy to pay for the displacement
while repairs are undertaken.  Has the landlord violated the RLTA by
demanding the tenant move out without offering to pay for the
displacement? Could this demand be considered constructive eviction by
the landlord? What rights and remedies does the tenant have against
such a demand?

** Summary

Under Washington's Residential Landlord-Tenant Act (RLTA), a landlord
who discovers severe mold and moisture damage not caused by the tenant
has a statutory duty to remedy the condition—not demand that the
tenant bear the cost of displacement. The landlord's verbal-only
demand that the tenant vacate immediately and use their own renter's
insurance to cover displacement costs implicates multiple violations
of the RLTA. Specifically: (1) the landlord has breached the implied
warranty of habitability by allowing such conditions to exist without
remedying them; (2) a verbal demand to vacate carries no legal weight
under the RLTA, which strictly requires written notices and just-cause
grounds for eviction; (3) shifting displacement costs to the tenant's
renter's insurance is not authorized by the RLTA and, if a
governmental condemnation order is issued, may violate RCW 59.18.085's
mandatory relocation assistance obligations; and (4) the demand may
constitute constructive eviction, provided the tenant actually vacates
the premises. The tenant has significant statutory remedies, including
the right to remain in possession, withhold or reduce rent, pursue
repair-and-deduct, terminate the lease without further obligation, and
sue for diminished rental value.

** Landlord's Duties Under the RLTA and the Implied Warranty of Habitability

Washington's RLTA codifies an implied warranty of habitability that
obligates landlords to "at all times during the tenancy keep the
premises fit for human habitation" WA ST 59.18.060. This general duty
is reinforced by a series of specific obligations relevant to mold and
moisture damage: the landlord must maintain structural
components—including roofs, floors, walls, chimneys, foundations, and
"all other structural components, in reasonably good repair so as to
be usable"—keep the dwelling "in reasonably weathertight condition,"
and maintain "all electrical, plumbing, heating, and other facilities
and appliances supplied by him or her in reasonably good working
order" WA ST 59.18.060. The landlord is also required to maintain the
premises in substantial compliance with applicable building, housing,
and health codes, and to provide tenants with information about the
health hazards of indoor mold WA ST 59.18.060.

Where the bathroom structure is severely damaged by moisture and mold,
and that damage is not attributable to the tenant's conduct, the
landlord is squarely within its statutory obligation to remedy the
condition under RCW 59.18.060 WA ST 59.18.060. The RLTA expressly
exempts the landlord from the duty to repair only "where the defective
condition complained of was caused by the conduct of such tenant, his
or her family, invitee, or other person acting under his or her
control" WA ST 59.18.060. Because the facts state the damage was not
caused by the tenant, no such exception applies.

The Supreme Court of Washington has emphasized that the RLTA was
enacted to "balance the bargaining positions between landlord and
tenant in residential leasing—most notably by placing affirmative
duties on the landlord, such as the warranty of habitability, and by
treating many of the parties' obligations as interdependent" Silver
v. Rudeen Management Company, Inc., 197 Wash.2d 535 (2021). The
statutory framework places the burden of maintaining habitable
conditions squarely on the landlord WA ST 59.18.060.

** The Landlord's Verbal Demand Is Legally Ineffective and Potentially Violates the RLTA

*** No Legally Valid Eviction Through Verbal Notice

Under the RLTA, a landlord may not require a tenant to vacate without
providing written notice that complies with the statute's specific
requirements. RCW 59.18.650 limits a landlord to evicting a
residential tenant based on an exclusive list of grounds and requires
any eviction notice to "Identify the facts and circumstances known and
available to the landlord at the time of the issuance of the notice
that support the cause or causes with enough specificity so as to
enable the tenant to respond and prepare a defense to any incidents
alleged" WA ST 59.18.650. Courts have held that both the RLTA and the
generalized unlawful detainer statutes are "strictly construed" in
favor of tenants Egbert v. Jorgensen, 36 Wash.App.2d 1
(2025). Critically, "[N]o landlord ... may ever use nonjudicial,
self-help methods to remove a tenant" Gray v. Pierce County Housing
Authority, 123 Wash.App. 744 (2004). A verbal demand to vacate—without
a written, legally compliant notice—is a form of unauthorized
self-help that carries no legal force under the RLTA and does not
obligate the tenant to leave the premises

*** Demanding the Tenant Use Renter's Insurance Contravenes the RLTA Framework

The RLTA does not authorize a landlord to shift the cost of
displacement to a tenant through the tenant's personal renter's
insurance policy. The statutory framework places the burden of
maintaining habitable conditions squarely on the landlord WA ST
59.18.060. When repairs require temporary vacancy, the financial
burden of displacement flows from the landlord's maintenance
obligation.

*** Relocation Assistance Obligations Under RCW 59.18.085

The RLTA's mandatory relocation assistance provision, RCW 59.18.085,
is triggered when "a governmental agency responsible for the
enforcement of a building, housing, or other appropriate code has
notified the landlord that a dwelling will be condemned or will be
unlawful to occupy due to the existence of conditions that violate
applicable codes, statutes, ordinances, or regulations," and the "a
landlord, who knew or should have known of the existence of these
conditions" WA ST 59.18.085. In that event, the landlord must pay
relocation assistance equal to "the greater amount of two thousand
dollars per dwelling unit or three times the monthly rent," and must
return all prepaid deposits and rent WA ST 59.18.085. This payment
must be made within seven days of the governmental agency's
condemnation or displacement order WA ST 59.18.085.

The Washington Court of Appeals confirmed in Pham v. Corbett, 187
Wash.App. 816 (2015) that once a landlord receives such governmental
notice, "there is no safe harbor for landlords...even if they are in
the process of permitting," because imposing a cure period "would
allow landlords to delay the process and continue to rent unlawful
dwellings without penalty" Pham v. Corbett, 187 Wash.App. 816
(2015). Importantly, however, the relocation assistance statute is not
triggered by the landlord's own verbal demand—it requires a formal
governmental notification of condemnation or unlawful occupancy status
WA ST 59.18.085 Pham v. Corbett, 187 Wash.App. 816 (2015).

If governmental authorities have not yet condemned or declared the
property unlawful to occupy, the explicit relocation assistance
obligations under RCW 59.18.085 have not yet arisen. Nevertheless, the
landlord's verbal demand to vacate may independently constitute
harassment or intimidation prohibited by the same statute, which bars
a landlord from "Evict, harass, or intimidate tenants into vacating
their units for the purpose of avoiding or diminishing application of
this section" the relocation assistance provisions during any period
of code violation WA ST 59.18.085. The demand that the tenant use
their own insurance rather than accept landlord-funded relocation
could be characterized as precisely such intimidation.

** Can the Landlord's Demand Constitute Constructive Eviction?

*** Elements of Constructive Eviction Under Washington Law

Washington courts define constructive eviction as "an intentional or
injurious interference by the landlord or those acting under his
authority" that "deprives the tenant of the means or the power of
beneficial enjoyment of the demised premises or any part thereof, or
materially impairs, such beneficial enjoyment" Old City Hall LLC
v. Pierce County AIDS Foundation, 181 Wash.App. 1 (2014) Myers
v. Western Farmers Ass'n, 75 Wash.2d 133 (1969). The Washington
Supreme Court articulated this standard in Coulos v. Desimone, 34
Wash.2d 87, 208 P.2d 105 (1949), and applied it in Myers v. Western
Farmers Ass'n, 75 Wash.2d 133 (1969), and the Court of Appeals applied
it comprehensively in Old City Hall LLC v. Pierce County AIDS
Foundation, 181 Wash.App. 1 (2014), where building conditions that had
become "unsanitary, unbearable, unworkable, and unsafe" were found to
constitute constructive eviction as a matter of law.

*** Analysis of the Landlord's Demand

A constructive eviction claim in this scenario rests on two potential
bases: (1) the underlying mold and moisture conditions themselves, and
(2) the landlord's verbal demand to vacate.

Regarding the conditions: Severe bathroom structural damage caused by
moisture and mold implicates structural components, plumbing systems,
and sanitation—precisely the conditions that Washington's RLTA
recognizes as substantially endangering or impairing the health or
safety of a tenant under RCW 59.18.115 WA ST 59.18.115. Conditions of
this severity, which the landlord has recognized are serious enough to
require the tenant to relocate entirely during repairs, can materially
impair the tenant's beneficial enjoyment of the premises.

Regarding the verbal demand: The demand itself—particularly when
delivered as an immediate, verbal ultimatum without notice, without
offering to pay displacement costs, and directing the tenant to use
their own insurance—may constitute an intentional or injurious
interference with the tenant's possession.

*** Critical Limitation: The Tenant Must Actually Vacate

Additionally, the tenant must provide the landlord with notice of
defective conditions and an opportunity to remedy them before claiming
constructive eviction Pague v. Petroleum Products, Inc., 77 Wash.2d
219 (1969). Abandoning the premises before providing such notice
waives the constructive eviction defense Old City Hall LLC v. Pierce
County AIDS Foundation, 181 Wash.App. 1 (2014).

** Tenant Rights and Remedies

*** Tenant's Right to Stay and Withhold Full Rent

The tenant is not legally required to vacate simply because the
landlord has verbally demanded it. The tenant may remain in possession
and withhold rent in excess of the diminished rental value of the
premises. Under RCW 59.18.110, a tenant "shall not be obligated to pay
rent in excess of the diminished rental value of the premises until
such defect or defects are corrected by the landlord" WA ST
59.18.110. The court may determine the diminution in rental value and
render judgment against the landlord for rent paid in excess of that
value from the time of notice of the defect WA ST 59.18.110.

*** Formal Notice and Repair Remedies

The tenant must first give written notice to the landlord under RCW
59.18.070, which requires the landlord to commence remedial action
within: (1) twenty-four hours where the condition deprives the tenant
of utilities or is imminently hazardous to life; (2) seventy-two hours
where the condition deprives the tenant of use of a refrigerator,
range and oven, or major plumbing fixture; or (3) ten days in all
other cases WA ST 59.18.070. After proper notice and expiration of the
applicable period, if the landlord fails to remedy the condition, the
tenant has several options under the RLTA:

**** Repair-and-Deduct

Under RCW 59.18.100, the tenant has two repair-and-deduct options: (1)
For repairs requiring licensed or registered persons (or responsible
persons if no licensing required), the tenant may contract for repairs
and deduct up to two months' rent per repair, with total deductions
not exceeding two months' rent in any twelve-month period; or (2) For
repairs not requiring licensed persons and costing no more than one
month's rent, the tenant may perform the repair themselves and deduct
up to one month's rent per repair, with total deductions not exceeding
one month's rent in any twelve-month period WA ST 59.18.100.

**** Lease Termination:

Under RCW 59.18.090, the tenant may terminate the rental agreement and
quit the premises upon written notice to the landlord "without further
obligation under the rental agreement," and is entitled to a pro rata
refund of prepaid rent and shall receive a full and specific statement
of the basis for retaining any of the deposit together with any refund
due in accordance with RCW 59.18.280 WA ST 59.18.090.

**** Escrow of Rent:

Under RCW 59.18.115, if the landlord's failures "substantially
endangers or impairs the health or safety of a tenant"—including
structural, plumbing, and sanitation defects directly exposing tenants
to risk of illness or injury—the tenant may, after providing written
notice and obtaining governmental certification, deposit rent into a
court escrow account rather than paying the landlord WA ST 59.18.115

**** Court Action for Damages:

Under RCW 59.18.090, the tenant may bring an action in an appropriate
court, or at arbitration if so agreed, for any remedy provided under
the RLTA or otherwise provided by law WA ST 59.18.090.

*** Anti-Retaliation Protection

If the tenant takes any of the above actions—such as complaining to a
government agency about the mold conditions or asserting their RLTA
rights—and the landlord then takes adverse action within ninety days,
RCW 59.18.250 creates a rebuttable presumption that the landlord's
action is retaliatory WA ST 59.18.250. Retaliatory actions under RCW
59.18.240 include eviction, increasing rent, reduction of services,
and increasing the tenant's obligations, when such actions are
intended primarily to retaliate against the tenant's good faith and
lawful acts WA ST 59.18.240. This protection is particularly relevant
where the landlord's verbal demand to vacate immediately follows the
tenant's discovery of conditions that would support reporting to a
housing or building code authority. A tenant who prevails on a
retaliation claim is entitled to recover costs of suit and reasonable
attorney's fees WA ST 59.18.250.

*** Constructive Eviction Damages

Should the tenant choose to vacate the premises in response to the
intolerable conditions and the landlord's demand, the tenant may
assert a constructive eviction claim, which prospectively releases the
tenant from the obligation to pay rent upon vacation Old City Hall LLC
v. Pierce County AIDS Foundation, 181 Wash.App. 1 (2014).

** Practical Implications

The tenant is in a significantly stronger legal position than the
landlord's verbal demand suggests. The tenant has no obligation to
comply with a verbal demand to vacate—it carries no legal force under
the RLTA. The tenant should document the conditions through
photographs, inspection reports, and written communications. The
tenant's most important first step is to send written notice to the
landlord under RCW 59.18.070 specifying the nature of the defective
conditions. This notice triggers the landlord's obligation to begin
repairs within the applicable period and also starts the clock for the
tenant's remedies. If the conditions are severe enough to warrant
governmental inspection, the tenant may contact local housing or
building code authorities; a governmental finding of violation or
condemnation would independently trigger the landlord's relocation
assistance obligation under RCW 59.18.085. The tenant should be
careful not to abandon the premises without first giving proper
notice, as doing so could waive both the constructive eviction defense
and remedies under the RLTA.

** Recent Developments

Washington courts have continued to reinforce tenant protections under
the RLTA. In Egbert v. Jorgensen, 36 Wash.App.2d 1 (2025), decided in
2025, the Court of Appeals reaffirmed that the RLTA is strictly
construed in favor of tenants. In Kiemle & Hagood Company v. Daniels,
26 Wash.App.2d 199 (2023), decided in 2023, the Court of Appeals,
citing Gray v. Pierce County Housing Authority, noted that landlords
are not authorized to engage in self-help evictions and held that
eviction notices must be sufficiently particular to allow a tenant to
respond and prepare a defense under RCW 59.18.650(6)(b). These
decisions reflect the RLTA's consistent trajectory toward robust
tenant protections, particularly against informal or procedurally
defective attempts to remove tenants from their homes.

** Commentary on This Question

Constructive eviction occurs when a landlord’s wrongful act or
omission substantially interferes with a tenant's beneficial enjoyment
of the leased premises, effectively depriving the tenant of possession
and forcing them to vacate. Essential elements include that the
landlord caused the uninhabitable condition, the tenant provided
notice and allowed reasonable time to remedy, and the tenant abandoned
the premises within a reasonable time thereafter. The landlord's
intent is irrelevant; liability arises from conduct rendering the
premises unusable, even without express eviction or physical
exclusion. Partial constructive eviction applies when only a portion
of the premises is uninhabitable, entitling the tenant to a partial
rent abatement or other relief, though courts may still require
payment of rent for the unaffected areas 70 AMJUR POF 3d 1, 43 AMJUR
POF 3d 329, 213 AMJUR POF 3d 245.

Mold and moisture-related damage can result in constructive eviction
if they render parts of the premises uninhabitable, as courts have
recognized the landlord's breach of implied warranty of habitability
in such contexts when tenants provided notice and allowed reasonable
time for repair. A tenant may terminate the lease and seek damages
including rent abatement, repair costs, and possibly emotional
distress damages. However, tenants bear the risk of proving the
landlord’s responsibility and the severity of conditions. Demands by
landlords for tenants to move immediately without offering relocation
assistance may fall within a constructive eviction framework if done
without proper remedies; tenants have counterclaims and defenses
available under such circumstances 43 AMJUR POF 3d 329, 118 COA2d 327,
42 AMJUR POF 2d 317.
